Hi! I'm Priya from Team Coyote. Yeah, ready to walk through Week 6.

The thing that stuck with me was the line between an observation and an insight. Specifically a moment in lecture where Magy contrasted 'the user clicked the back button' (observation) with 'the user lost trust in the page' (insight). I had been writing all our field notes as observations and calling them findings, which now feels lazy.

Concrete moment — in our Tuesday synthesis session, Marco pointed at a sticky that said 'participant returned to homepage three times' and asked 'so what?' Nothing more on that one — let's move on.

1.2. Methods in Practice

We did affinity diagramming on Wednesday for about three hours, with maybe 180 stickies from our user interviews. Theory said cluster by behavior; practice was way messier — we kept defaulting to clustering by topic ('signup flow', 'navigation', 'errors') because that's how our notes were already organized.

Definitely hit the 'clustering by topic instead of behavior' pitfall, and 'one person driving the wall' for the first 45 minutes — Devi was moving stickies faster than the rest of us could read them, and we had to literally tell her to step back. Once she did, the clusters got more honest. We also went abstract too fast on one cluster called 'frustration' — too generic, eventually broke it into three behavior-rooted ones.

Move on, yeah.

1.3. Knowledge Shift: Before vs. After

I thought affinity diagramming was basically grouping similar quotes — the kind of thing you could do in a spreadsheet in an hour. I treated it like coding qualitative data with a fancier name.

I was surprised by how much the *physical* act of moving the sticky mattered — a sticky in your hand on the wall is something you have to defend by where you put it. In a spreadsheet you just type a category and move on. The friction of moving the sticky forced us to actually argue.

I'm still uncertain when to stop clustering. We made it through three rounds of regrouping and I genuinely could not tell whether round three was insight or just fatigue.

Move on.

1.4. Connection to the Capstone Project

It changes how I'm going to write our next round of interview questions. Up until now I was writing questions that surface attitudes — what users think — and treating those as primary. After this week I think we need at least half our questions to be behavioral probes that get them to walk through what they actually did, because the gap between say-vs-do is where the real insights are.

Smallest concrete change: in next week's interviews I'll add a 'walk me through the last time you actually did X' prompt to every question that currently asks 'what do you think about X'. Concrete and easy to check on Friday.

Move on.
